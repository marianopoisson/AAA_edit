\abstract{
Quasars and Active Galactic Nuclei (AGNs) are key agents in galaxy evolution, capable of ionizing the intergalactic medium, heating the circumgalactic halo, suppressing star formation, and driving large-scale gas outflows. The peak of quasar activity occurred at $z \approx 2$--3, where rest-frame optical diagnostics such as [O\,\textsc{iii}]~$\lambda5007$ and Balmer lines (e.g., H$\alpha$, H$\beta$) shift into the near-infrared (NIR). These lines are essential for estimating bolometric luminosities, AGN classification (type~1 vs.\ type~2), and identifying ionized outflows through line broadening and asymmetries. However, NIR flux calibration is difficult due to time-variable telluric absorption and emission. Standard practice relies on observations of telluric standards, which are not true spectrophotometric standards, and uncertainties increase when combining data from multiple nights with varying atmospheric conditions. To quantify these effects, a $z=2.264$ quasar was observed with Flamingos-2 on Gemini South in $H$ and $K$ bands. The $K$-band data, capturing H$\alpha$, were obtained over three nights with different telluric calibrators. Flux calibration was performed using three methods: photometric magnitudes, a spectrophotometric standard, and sensitivity functions from telluric stars. The impact on emission line fluxes, broad component detection, and derived bolometric luminosity is evaluated. This study highlights NIR calibration limitations that affect our understanding of high-redshift AGNs and their role in galaxy evolution.

}
